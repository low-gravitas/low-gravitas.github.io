I don't use LLMs to write articles. That's probably the first thing worth saying. Everything published here is written by me, and I intend to keep it that way. What I do use them for is the work around and behind articles — features, tooling, specific projects.

I also let them suggest edits to things I've written, mostly copy-editing. I'm a decent editor but nowhere near a professional one. Having a second pass before I publish gives me more confidence, and hopefully means fewer typos make it out the door. Same for fact-checking — if I'm making a claim I'm not certain about, it's a useful sanity check.

On the project side: I used Claude Code to help build the Symbol Font and its site. I'd already created the Low Gravitas Zen theme years ago, but LLMs helped me update it recently and put together its site as well. They let me do in a few hours what would have taken days. One area where they've been especially useful is accessibility — without them I probably would have cut corners I shouldn't cut. They make mistakes, of course. So do I.

---

Why am I okay with this given the ethical and moral gray area? Several reasons. I'm not going to make a slippery slope argument, but living in the US — and especially making a living here, even before the last ten years or so — has always involved some degree of moral and ethical compromise. I do have lines I don't cross. LLMs don't hit those for me. And if they did, then at least the last ten or twenty years of my career would have as well.

The thing I think about most is intellectual property — specifically the theft of it. The argument that someone using LLMs to generate art, code, or anything else is displacing a person who might have been paid to do that work is real. But I don't think it's the central problem, and here's why: the things that don't require humans to make or invent have always been replaced by machines built by humans. That's just the pattern. And alongside that pattern, we consistently go back to — or put a premium on — things that are human-made. Consider woodworking. Almost everyone appreciates a hand-carved piece more than a factory-made one. Consider clothing. The best, most expensive, most fashionable clothes are hand-stitched and tailored by people. The list is long.

Why is this? Let me steer back to LLMs specifically and then get back to that question. LLMs are not true "artificial intelligence" in the sense that they can achieve personhood. They can iterate and achieve things that are very impressive. They can even solve problems humans have not been able to. But they do not truly invent new concepts. They can mash things together, but they don't have the taste to know when that is good. This, in part, is because they don't have emotion, though they can fake it. They don't have, for lack of a better word, a soul. But honestly, that's always been a fuzzily defined concept at best. How would we know if they did?

The question at the end of that paragraph answers the question at the beginning of it. If we truly preferred what the machines made for us over hand-crafted or human-involved creations, then we could argue that the machines have matched or surpassed us. Until then, there is no problem of "flooding the market with slop" in that sense. There are other risks associated with "slop", but we were headed in that direction before LLMs came to be. If anything, I expect their existence to speed us along the road to being able to deal with that problem.
